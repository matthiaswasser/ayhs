Context/Intro

  • Gender has a history - it isn't just natural
  • Basically all societies have divided people up into groups that include "male," "female," and sometimes additional categories, and then assigned expectations and roles to those categories
  • Both the expectations and categories have changed over time
  • In the last century there's been an incomplete but massive shift towards less patriarchy (domination of men over women), and in your lifetimes there's been a fairly rapid shift towards a more open category system

Who cares?

Proverbially - if anyone wants to fact-check this for me they can - Biden running in 2020 was asked by a conservative journalist, looking for a gotcha: "How many genders are there?" and replied with "at least three."

As a slightly annoyed response to a slightly memey question, whose main goal was probably to move on without ceding any ground to the hostile journalist or upsetting any of his younger supporters, this is pretty funny.

But I think it's also as good an answer as any. When historians talk about "gender," they're less concerned with body parts than social expectations, and social expectations differ (duh) by society. Different societies have grouped people up differently, and in particular, pretty much every society has a division we can call "gender" - that is, a set of categories that includes "male" and "female" and sometimes some other categories, like the Navajo n√°dleehi or Albanian burrnesha. It's as useless to argue over which of these are real as to argue over which alphabet is real.

And not only can the categories change over time - for instance, it's in your lifetimes that Americans started recognizing "nonbinary" as a category - but also of course the expectations of what a man is, what tasks he has to do and what virtues he has to embody, and so on. For instance, in many parts of the 1700s and 1800s America, it was considered degrading for a man to work for wages - a role not much better than a slave, letting someone else boss you around! Nobler to starve! By the 1900s it was established that a man was a failure if he wasn't working for wages.

Gender includes superficial rules like "men but not women are expected to wear neckties to white-collar work" or "women but not men can paint their nails" (both of these are weakening currently.) But like the economy and like foreign policy and like civil rights, gender is historically interesting because it's about power. We'll see that expectations of women and men are often pretty sensitive to who has leverage - the ability to set terms in a relationship because they can say "if not, I'll walk, and then you'll be worse off." Relationships between employers and employees work the same way. You don't have to actually start dictating terms for leverage to be useful - often if you're in a situation with little leverage people will start taking advantage of you because they can get away with it, and when you get a little leverage, they start being way more polite. This isn't even the result of conscious cruelty - just people taking the easiest path at any given moment. And these power differences can get enshrined into custom and other little behaviors that reinforce subordination. (Think of how I call you by your first names and you call me by my last name - that's a pretty typical expression of power and hierarchy.)

In the reading below you'll read one particular instance of how leverage intersects with the gender division of labor, in one particular household.

Vocabulary

gender
how societies divide people up in to male/female/(sometimes other) categories and create/enforce expectations around that
gender division of labor
who is expected to do what jobs based on gender
leverage
how much power you have in a relationship, based on what the other person loses if you stop cooperating
hierarchy
formalized power relationships, especially when they're in layers (kings over dukes over barons over knights over serfs, say)

Gender History in the Long Run

Context/Intro

  • Foraging societies largely have gender equality
  • Farming societies were mostly patriarchal, placing men in positions of power, including over their own wives and children
  • High childhood mortality required high birth rates to keep up, which in turn affected gender roles
  • Industrial societies have shifted towards less patriarchy over time

Why am I learning about this in an American history class?

For context!

An important way in which nature does matter

Here's a pretty depressing chart - or maybe a pretty optimistic chart, if you put the focus on what we've managed to accomplish:

A chart of childhood mortality, running around half until relatively recently

Let's set aside the massive tragedy, and just think about the math - how many kids does each woman need to have just to keep population constant? Four. So if a new mother spends 15 months nursing - she spends more than that in most traditional societies - that's a minimum of 8 years of life, on average, she spends pregnant or nursing.

In fact, when the categories are divided up, "women" in most societies means "the people we're presuming are going to be pregnant or nursing for most of the prime of their lives."

Foraging societies

By "foraging," I mean "hunter-gatherer." Most of the human timeline is us having this kind of economy, and most of it is pretty equal, both generally and in terms of the relationship between genders.

This surprises many people who've been trained to expect a model of "primitive" humans are ruled, like other great apes, by "alpha males," the biggest and most aggressive men around. That isn't too far off from how some of our closest relatives, like chimpanzees, operate - one guy who can push anybody around because he can take anyone in a fight. But men who act like that in human forager societies tend to get scolded by the community and asked to take themselves down a notch, and those who don't revise it from there can be ganged up on and killed, in their sleep if necessary. Thus, forager societies tend to rarely feature anybody who bosses anyone else around. Further, since land is mostly unguarded - full of food for anybody willing to put in the effort - if someone does start trying to boss you around, you can just leave.

That said, there's still some significant variation in this. In particular, some foraging societies are relatively more patriarchal - men have higher status than women, and men can boss around their wives. This seems to be most common when societies are patrilocal - where a wife moves to live in her husband's village. (People tend not to marry in the same village because that would lead to inbreeding before too long.) In a village like that, all the men know and are related to each other, but all the women are strangers, so the men will tend to team up and act as one team. This is useful if there's a risk of war - you want all the local young men to fight together, even die for each other, to defend the village - but maybe not so great if your husband beats you when you don't do what he wants (who would you turn to?)

If patrilocality leads to patriarchal societies, does matrilocality lead to matriarchal societies? Apparently not - maybe men's greater strength protects them here. But it does lead to fairly equal ones. As do other systems.

Herding societies

Herding societies tend to be very patriarchal. Why?

  1. Herds are pretty easy to steal. So for defensive reasons most herding societies are very patrilocal, consisting of men who know how to fight together and will always take each others' side.
  2. If you can get good at fighting like that, you can start enslaving people who aren't as good at fighting, like farmers. Enslaved women may be taken as enslaved wives, and by then extreme patriarchy is pretty entrenched.
